1 Where we are

In Module 1 we followed a packet from your browser, across the internet, to a website — say a bank's web server. It arrives. But arriving isn't the same as arriving safely. In this module we ask: how do we secure data as it travels over the open internet?

2 Assurance ①: no one can read your data

You open examplebank.com and type your username and password. Those credentials travel back over the open session — and anyone in the middle, tapping the wire or listening to the Wi-Fi, can read them straight off the network. That's a man-in-the-middle attack. Now toggle the fix:

The man in the middle — with and without encryption

The channel is open: the attacker reads your credentials in plaintext, then logs into your account.

That's the fix in action. The mechanics behind it — what encryption actually is, and the two kinds you'll meet everywhere — are below; expand what you want to go deeper on, then continue to assurance ②.

Encryption converts readable plaintext into scrambled ciphertext; decryption reverses it. Even if a man-in-the-middle captures the ciphertext, they can't recover the original message — it's like sending the message through a tunnel: visible that something passed, unreadable what it was. Every scheme has two pieces: an algorithm and a key.

A simple shift like this is cracked instantly. Real systems use complex algorithms — AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) or RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) — but the two ingredients are always the same: an algorithm and a key.

Two kinds of encryption

Encryption comes in two flavours, and the difference is best felt by hand. A symmetric scheme is a lock with one key — both sides must hold a copy. An asymmetric scheme is a lock with a pair of keys, where what one locks, only the other unlocks. Try both:

How TLS uses both
Asymmetric encryption is slow, so it isn't used for the whole conversation. Instead it's used once — to safely deliver a shared symmetric key — and the fast symmetric key encrypts everything after. You'll see exactly this in the handshake below.

3 Assurance ②: you're talking to the real bank

Encryption hides the message — but how do you know the public key really belongs to your bank, and not an attacker? You could be sent to a fake bank site by a phishing email, or by DNS spoofing (the DNS lookup resolves examplebank.com to a bad, attacker-controlled IP address instead of the real one). Encryption to the wrong party is no protection at all.

The answer is a digital certificate. The bank gives its public key and identifying details to a Certificate Authority (CA) — a small set of highly trusted organizations (e.g. DigiCert, Let's Encrypt). The CA verifies the bank is who it claims to be, then signs the certificate with the CA's own private key. Your browser ships with the CAs' public keys, so it can validate the signature and trust the bank's public key inside.

Why not skip the CA?
Think of applying to a university: you can't mail your own grades and expect them to count — your school sends them, because a trusted third party vouches for them. A bank can't vouch for its own identity either; the CA's signature is what makes the certificate trustworthy. A valid certificate is what produces the padlock in your browser.

4 Putting it together: the TLS handshake

Both assurances now combine, in three moves: first, get the bank's public key (from its certificate — assurance ② proven); second, agree on a symmetric session key (delivered safely using that public key); third, encrypt everything else with the session key (assurance ①, at full speed). Step through it:

TLS handshake: HTTP → HTTPS

HTTP vs HTTPS — send the same password over both

Same HTTP request, same password — the only difference is the TLS layer wrapped around the right-hand connection. That single layer is the "S" in HTTPS.

Adding this encryption layer on top of the transport connection is called TLS (Transport Layer Security). HTTP carried over TLS is HTTPS — the "S" is Secure. Both sides have now agreed on the encryption key and the encryption algorithm — the two ingredients every encryption scheme needs.

The exchange above — the client encrypting the session key with the server's public key — is the classic TLS 1.2 story, and the clearest way to learn the idea. Today's standard, TLS 1.3, derives the session key differently (both sides compute it together via a Diffie–Hellman exchange, so the key itself never crosses the wire at all). The trust logic is unchanged: certificate → CA validation → asymmetric setup → fast symmetric session.

5 The bigger picture: the CIA triad

The two assurances you just built are part of a standard framework. Security professionals group all protection goals into three:

  • Confidentiality — no one in the middle can read the conversation. (Solved by encryption.)
  • Integrity — no one can alter the message (“send $1,000” must not become “send $100,000”), and we're talking to the right party. (Solved by signatures and certificates.)
  • Availability — the other party is reachable when we need it.
