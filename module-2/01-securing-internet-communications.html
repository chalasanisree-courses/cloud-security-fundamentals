Securing Internet Communications

Module 2 Security foundations Encryption & TLS
Learning Objectives
  • Explain why ordinary internet communication is insecure (the man-in-the-middle risk)
  • Describe symmetric vs asymmetric encryption and the key-sharing problem each one creates or solves
  • Explain how digital certificates and Certificate Authorities establish trust in a server's identity
  • Trace the TLS handshake that turns an open HTTP connection into secure HTTPS

1 Where we are

In Module 1, we got a packet from your browser to a bank's web server: the URL resolved through DNS, a TCP three-way handshake opened a session, and IP carried the packets hop by hop. That works — but the channel is wide open. Now we ask the next question: how do we secure it?

2 The threat: man-in-the-middle

You open chase.com and type your username and password. Those credentials travel back over the open session — and anyone in the middle, tapping the wire or listening to the Wi-Fi, can read them straight off the network.

A man-in-the-middle attack

Man-in-the-middle (MITM)
An attacker positioned between you and the server reads (or alters) traffic you assume is private. Sending credentials as plaintext over a shared medium is all it takes. The fix is to make the contents unreadable in transit — encryption.

3 What encryption is

Encryption converts readable plaintext into scrambled ciphertext; decryption reverses it. Even if a man-in-the-middle captures the ciphertext, they can't recover the original message. Every scheme has two pieces: an algorithm and a key.

A simple shift like this is cracked instantly. Real systems use complex algorithms — AES (Advanced Encryption Standard), 3DES, or RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) — but the two ingredients are always the same: an algorithm and a key.

4 Symmetric encryption — and its problem

The simplest model uses one shared key for both encryption and decryption — like a lock with a single key that both parties hold. You and the bank agree on a key ahead of time; you encrypt your credentials with it, the bank decrypts with the same key. It's fast and simple.

But there are two problems. First, the key-sharing chicken-and-egg problem: to communicate securely you first need a shared key — but how do you send the key itself securely over the same untrusted internet? Second, because both parties hold the key, it's twice as exposed: if either side loses it, the protection is gone.

Symmetric encryption: one shared key

5 Asymmetric encryption

Asymmetric encryption solves the key-sharing problem with a key pair: two mathematically linked keys where what one locks, only the other unlocks. The receiver keeps one key private (never shared) and publishes the other as a public key that anyone may use.

To send the bank a secret, you grab the bank's public key and encrypt with it. Only the bank's private key can decrypt — and the bank never shares that. So a man-in-the-middle, holding only the public key, can't read anything. Crucially, no shared secret ever crosses the wire. This is the basis of PKI (Public Key Infrastructure), which underlies all secure internet communication.

The trade-off that shapes everything next
Symmetric is fast but hard to key-share; asymmetric is easy to key-share but slow. The real internet uses asymmetric encryption once — just to exchange a symmetric key — then switches to fast symmetric encryption for the rest of the session. Hold that thought for the TLS handshake below.

6 Digital certificates & Certificate Authorities

Encryption hides the message — but how do you know the public key really belongs to your bank, and not an attacker? You could be sent to a fake bank site by a phishing email, or by DNS spoofing (the DNS lookup resolves chase.com to a bad, attacker-controlled IP address instead of the real one). Encryption to the wrong party is no protection at all.

The answer is a digital certificate. The bank gives its public key and identifying details to a Certificate Authority (CA) — a small set of highly trusted organizations (e.g. DigiCert, VeriSign). The CA verifies the bank is who it claims to be, then signs the certificate with the CA's own private key. Your browser ships with the CAs' public keys, so it can validate the signature and trust the bank's public key inside.

Why not skip the CA?
Think of applying to a university: you can't mail your own grades and expect them to count — your school sends them, because a trusted third party vouches for them. A bank can't vouch for its own identity either; the CA's signature is what makes the certificate trustworthy. A valid certificate is what produces the padlock in your browser.

8 From securing the channel to the CIA triad

Step back from the bank example. Any two parties communicating across a channel they don't control need a few basic assurances:

  • Confidentiality — no one in the middle can read the conversation. (Solved by encryption.)
  • Integrity — no one can alter the message (“send $1,000” must not become “send $100,000”), and we're talking to the right party. (Solved by signatures and certificates.)
  • Availability — the other party is reachable when we need it.
