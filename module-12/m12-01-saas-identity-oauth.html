Buyer, not Builder: Identity, OAuth & OIDC

Module 12 SaaS pivot OAuth · OIDC
In this note

  • Why “identity is the new perimeter” once the network perimeter is gone

1 The pivot: builder → buyer

For most of this course you were a builder — spinning up servers, patching, configuring firewalls. SaaS makes you a buyer: you just consume the app. The stack you manage shrinks dramatically. Toggle:

Identity is the new perimeter
In SaaS the app sits on the public internet — there’s no firewall or DMZ around it. Often the only thing between an attacker and your data is a login screen. That’s why MFA and strong identity matter so much here: a stolen password shouldn’t be enough on its own.

2 OAuth: don’t hand over your password

You want Spotify to read a playlist from your Google account. The wrong move is to give Spotify your Google password — now it can do anything. OAuth hands it a valet key instead. Compare:

Master key vs valet key

The four players
Every OAuth flow has four: the resource owner (you), the client (the app, e.g. Spotify), the authorization server (issues the token, e.g. Google Auth), and the resource server (holds the data, e.g. Google Drive). The access token is the valet key; the scopes stamped on it are exactly what it’s allowed to do.

3 OIDC: who you are vs what you can do

OAuth proves what an app may do — not who you are. “Log in with Google” needs identity, so OIDC adds an ID token on top of OAuth. Two tokens, two jobs — flip them:

ID token vs access token

OIDC vs SAML
Both let you log in through a third party (federated identity). OIDC uses lightweight JSON / JWT — the modern standard for mobile apps and “Log in with Google” buttons. SAML uses heavier XML — the older enterprise standard you’ll meet in corporate SSO and legacy systems.
Remember

SaaS turns you from a builder into a buyer: the vendor owns the infrastructure, OS, and network, so your job shrinks to identity and data. With no network perimeter, identity is the new perimeter — lean on MFA. OAuth is delegated authorization: it gives an app a scoped access token (a valet key) so it never sees your password — four players (owner, client, auth server, resource server), with scopes limiting the token. OIDC adds delegated authentication with an ID token (a JWT of claims: who you are) — the access token says what you may do, the ID token says who you are. OIDC is JSON/modern; SAML is XML/enterprise.
