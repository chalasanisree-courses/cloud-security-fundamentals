Evolution of the Internet

Module 1 Foundations Lecture 1
Learning Objectives
  • Explain why pre‑internet computing left organizations as isolated information islands
  • Trace the five eras of internet evolution — from the pre‑internet world of isolated mainframes — and the problem each one solved
  • Identify the foundational protocols — TCP/IP, then HTTP, SMTP, FTP — and when they emerged
  • Connect each era's expansion to the steadily growing security surface this course must defend

1 Where we are

This is the first lecture of the course, and it deliberately starts before security. Everything we will defend — networks, hosts, data, identities — rides on the internet, so we begin by understanding how the internet came to be and why it looks the way it does today. The whole story fits in one timeline below: the pre‑internet world, then five eras of evolution. The key idea is that each era solved a problem the previous one created — while quietly enlarging the attack surface.

2 The timeline

Click each stage to expand what changed — the 1990s and 2020s stages carry extra depth (the Web protocols and the AI stack).

Interactive timeline — pre‑internet + five eras

  • Computers were mainframes — massive machines filling entire rooms, owned by large institutions like universities and governments. Users connected through dumb terminals.
  • Mainframes at different institutions were wired together only in proprietary ways — no standard way for them to talk to each other.
  • The result: information silos — islands of data that couldn't reach one another. Every era that follows removes another barrier.
  • Computers became affordable — people owned them at home, not just institutions.
  • Machines needed a standard way to connect. Proprietary mainframe links gave way to TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol).
  • TCP/IP became the backbone of the internet. This is the moment the internet truly begins.
  • Connecting computers only matters if they can exchange information. The Web's protocols made that possible: HTTP, SMTP, FTP.
  • Search engines (Google) made the flood of new information findable.
  • Browser wars (Microsoft vs Netscape) — a fight to own the client end of the experience.

HTTP = HyperText Transfer Protocol · SMTP = Simple Mail Transfer Protocol · FTP = File Transfer Protocol.

Whoever owned the browser shaped the user's entire internet experience — which is what the browser wars were really about. Google ultimately controlled both ends: the client (Chrome's leading share) and the server (search).

  • Landlines gave way to mobile networks — cellular (2G/3G/LTE) and Wi-Fi.
  • Smartphones (Apple, Android) made that mobility useful.
  • Cloud applications arrived: store files in Dropbox instead of your disk, use Gmail instead of a local mail app, Facebook, Amazon. The internet was reliable enough that apps no longer had to live on your device.
  • The things connected to the internet were no longer just PCs and phones.
  • TVs, thermostats, fans, appliances — all online, all backed by cloud apps.
  • Netflix streams to your TV; Google Nest controls your thermostat. Everyday objects became internet endpoints.
  • AI now improves experiences across the whole internet: fraud detection in banking, personalized recommendations in shopping, chatbots in customer service. Those are AI applications.
  • To see where AI sits underneath those applications, compare the traditional computing stack with the AI stack — the layers line up, but the middle changes.

The Platform layer of the traditional stack is replaced by the Model & Orchestration layer — the new heart of the stack, where the large language models (LLMs) live. Applications sit on top of it; infrastructure (GPUs and cloud) sits beneath it. That middle layer is the one to remember — it's what makes the stack “AI.”

3 The through-line — and why it matters for security

Read top to bottom, the eras are a single argument: each layer was added onto the one before it.

◈ Each era added surface areaMore connectivity, more to defend ▶

Standard connections (TCP/IP) made the internet possible → standard information exchange (the Web) made it useful → mobility (networks + devices) made it everywhere → the cloud made applications location‑independent → IoT connected everything → AI now sits on top, improving every experience.

Every one of those steps added more endpoints, more data in motion, and more things an attacker can reach. That expanding surface is exactly why the rest of this course exists.

Remember
The internet is not one invention — it's a stack of standards, each layered on the one before. Pre‑internet = isolated islands; the whole story is the progressive removal of barriers between machines. Convenience and exposure grew together.
