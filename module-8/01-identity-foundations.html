1 From many logins to one

Every app used to keep its own database of usernames and passwords. That meant many credentials to remember and many places to deprovision a leaver. The fix: single sign-on through a central identity provider. Flip between them:

Siloed logins vs single sign-on

The broker idea
The identity provider (Duo, Okta, Microsoft Entra) becomes a trusted broker. You authenticate once with it; it then vouches for you to Canvas, Zoom, and the rest — so those apps never handle your password at all.

2 Three pillars of identity

“Logging in” is really three separate jobs. Walk a user from the street into the room — click each gate:

Authentication · access · authorization

Who owns each pillar
The IdP usually owns the first two — verifying you and deciding whether to let you into an app. Authorization (what you can do inside) has traditionally been the application’s job. Bob and the instructor both enter Canvas; only the instructor can build a quiz.

3 Trusting a token you didn’t issue

The IdP hands the user a token — a note that says “this user is authenticated.” But the user carries it to the app, so the app must be sure it’s real and unaltered. Digital signatures do that. Try tampering with it:

Sign at the IdP, verify at the app

Why only the hash is signed
The IdP signs a hash (small fingerprint) of the token, not the whole thing — signature schemes can only sign small amounts of data. The app re-hashes what it received and compares: match → trust; mismatch → reject. This proves both origin (the IdP’s private key signed it) and integrity (nothing changed).

4 Federation: trust across organizations

What if a Foothill student needs a De Anza app? Their identities live in different systems. Federation links them — and how you wire the trust decides whether it scales. Compare the three:

Three federation models

Remember

Apps stopped storing passwords by trusting a central identity provider for single sign-on. Identity is three jobs: authentication (who are you?), access management (may you enter this app?), and authorization (what may you do inside?) — the IdP owns the first two, the app traditionally owns the third. The app trusts the IdP’s token because it’s digitally signed: the IdP signs a hash with its private key, the app verifies with the IdP’s public key and re-checks the hash — proving origin and integrity. And federation extends trust across organizations, scaling best through a central broker.
