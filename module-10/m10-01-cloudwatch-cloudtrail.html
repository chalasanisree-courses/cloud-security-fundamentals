CloudWatch & CloudTrail

Module 10 Logging · monitoring Auditing
In this note
  • Two different questions: “how is the app behaving?” vs “how are people behaving?”
  • CloudWatch — metrics, logs, and alarms for operational health
  • CloudTrail — the audit trail of who did what, and why compliance needs it
  • The data-events trap: the read-only attacker default CloudTrail can’t see

1 Two very different questions

“Logging” covers two jobs that sound similar but answer opposite questions. AWS splits them into two services. Flip between them:

CloudWatch vs CloudTrail

The one-line split
CloudWatch watches the application; CloudTrail watches the people. “Is the server running slowly?” is a CloudWatch question. “Did Bob delete the database?” is a CloudTrail question.

2 CloudWatch: is everything healthy?

CloudWatch collects logs (an NGINX line per request: client IP, status code, latency) and metrics (numbers like CPU %). You set a baseline for normal and an alarm for when a metric crosses a threshold. Drag the CPU:

An alarm at 80% CPU

CPU utilization: 35%

Logs are for defense too
The NGINX log isn’t just for troubleshooting a crash. If you spot a single IP sending thousands of requests, that’s a denial-of-service attempt — the log tells you exactly which IP to block.

3 CloudTrail: who did what?

CloudTrail records user activity — every API call — as an audit trail. When something goes wrong, it answers four questions:

An audit-trail entry

Why this is a legal requirement
Regulations like HIPAA (health records), PCI DSS (card data), and GDPR (personal data) require you to prove who accessed sensitive data. Without an audit trail to show inspectors, an organisation can face large fines. (Note: CloudTrail enables that proof — turning it on isn’t the same as being compliant.)

4 The data-events trap

Here’s the blind spot every student must know. CloudTrail does not record everything by default. Choose what the attacker does:

Will default CloudTrail catch it?

What “logged by default” really means
Management events (actions that change your environment’s structure — CreateBucket, DeleteTable, AddUser) appear in CloudTrail’s free 90-day Event history. Data events (high-volume reads inside a resource — GetObject, GetItem) are off by default and cost extra to enable. So a thief who only reads your data can be invisible.
Remember

Two services, two questions. CloudWatch watches the application — logs and metrics for operational health, with alarms that fire when a metric (like CPU > 80%) crosses a threshold and can notify a person or take action. CloudTrail watches the people — an audit trail of who did what, when, and from where, the backbone of compliance (HIPAA, PCI DSS, GDPR). The trap: CloudTrail logs management events by default (in the 90-day history) but not data events — so a read-only attacker who never changes structure can slip by unless you’ve explicitly enabled data-event logging.
